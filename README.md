An econometric study examining how the COVID-19 pandemic influenced higher education decisions across U.S. regions, using data from the U.S. Census Bureau's Household Pulse Survey (August–September 2020).
Research Question: How did regional disparities during the COVID-19 pandemic affect individuals' choices regarding higher education attainment?
Key Features:

OLS linear probability model with three specifications and robustness checks
318,372 observations from the U.S. Census Bureau Household Pulse Survey
Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) regional dummy variables for granular geographic analysis
Controls for age, race, gender, household size, income, employment status, and education level
Time fixed effects across survey weeks 13–15 (August–September 2020)

Key Findings:

Middle-income households and larger family sizes positively correlated with academic plan changes
Older age, white racial identity, and male gender associated with fewer adjustments
Far West and Southwest respondents significantly more likely to alter plans than Southeast respondents
Regional policy environments and demographic composition emerged as key drivers of variation

Tools Used: R, OLS Regression, Bureau of Economic Analysis regional framework, U.S. Census Household Pulse Survey
Data Source: U.S. Census Bureau Household Pulse Survey — github.com/ashtonamburgey99
